Case Study

Yellow is the new Green

Transforming a fragmented, Excel-based financial planning process into an interactive dashboard that helps decision-makers understand, explore and trust their data.

Overview

From scattered data to one source of truth.

Financial planning at SECO's Economic Development and Cooperation division relied on manually maintained Excel files, screenshots and repeated explanations. Preparing for budget discussions often took days, and information quickly became outdated. The goal of the project was to create a single, interactive source of truth that would make financial data easier to access, understand and act upon.

Product

An interactive financial cockpit supporting the planning and monitoring of an annual budget of over CHF 400 million.

Users

Section Heads, Financial Controllers, Heads of Cooperation and Project Managers across SECO's Economic Cooperation and Development Division.

Team

Individual project

Problem

Important decisions were slowed down by fragmented information.

Time consuming

Users spent days preparing information before they could actually analyze and discuss it.

Limited exploration

Users could not easily filter, compare or drill into the data without creating additional views and calculations.

Limited trust

Because information was spread across several files, users often questioned whether they were working with the latest and most reliable data.

Role

End-to-end ownership of the product, from user research and UX/UI design to Power BI development, rollout and continuous iteration.

Constraints

With no prior Power BI experience, I designed and developed the solution alongside my regular responsibilities, requiring continuous learning and rapid upskilling throughout the project.

01

Research

From assumptions to insights.

Although I already knew the users and their workflows well, I wanted to challenge my assumptions and better understand how they interacted with financial information. Mapping user journeys helped reveal recurring frustrations, uncover different needs across user groups and define the main pain points that ultimately shaped the dashboard.

Challenge

There were several user groups with different needs. Designing for everyone equally risked creating a dashboard that was too generic.

Solution

Map user types, identify primary users and prioritize the needs of the people who drive planning and decision-making, rather than those of the largest user group.

02

Design

Designing for clarity.

Financial data can easily feel dense and intimidating. The visual design therefore played a key role in making complex information approachable and supporting quick decision-making. Working within a public-sector context, I opted for a clean and restrained visual language inspired by existing Swiss government dashboards, prioritizing clarity, accessibility and trust over decoration.

Challenge

Making a CHF 400M financial planning process understandable at a glance while preserving the level of detail required for operational decision-making, all within the technical constraints of Power BI.

Solution

Careful use of visual hierarchy, filtering, progressive disclosure, color coding and interactive drill-downs allowed users to move seamlessly between strategic overviews and detailed project information. Every design decision was continuously validated against Power BI's technical constraints to ensure that the final experience could be implemented as intended.

Using paper to generate ideas

Design decisions

Build trust through consistency

Consistent layouts, standardized metrics and a single source of truth help users understand the data faster and increase confidence in the information.

Optimize for high-impact users

Although several user groups used the dashboard, the experience was primarily designed around the needs of the people driving planning and budget decisions. Secondary users could still access the information they needed through filtering and drill-downs.

Leaving no one behind

Specific filters for non-primary user groups were added to ensure that all users could access the information they needed.

Choosing the right graphics

Preserving existing graphics where appropriate, while adding new ones where they added value.

Less color, more clarity

Rather than adopting the full departmental color palette, a restrained set of neutral tones was used to create a calm, data-focused interface. Federal red remains the only accent color, drawing attention where it matters without creating unnecessary visual clutter.

Make every pixel count

As the dashboard was intended for internal use, strict adherence to the official design system was not required. A more compact typeface was chosen to accommodate longer project titles.

03

Develop

Learning by doing.

Power BI was completely new territory for me. Through online training and extensive hands-on experimentation, I successfully delivered the solution while continuing my regular responsibilities. Introducing a new technology also required extensive stakeholder communication, change management and the creation of new access and governance processes around the dashboard. Within a year, I had become a recognized point of contact for dashboard design and Power BI within the organization.

Challenge

Bringing ambitious design ideas to life in an unfamiliar platform while introducing a new technology, defining governance processes and ensuring the final product could fully replace an existing business-critical process.

Solution

Breaking complex features into smaller problems, leveraging expert support and AI whenever technical challenges arose, and ensuring stakeholder buy-in through transparent communication, early involvement and continuous support.

04

Test

Does it actually work?

A moderated usability study with one section head and one project manager uncovered four key improvements before launch. Four months later, a questionnaire was distributed to section heads to evaluate adoption, usability and satisfaction. Ten out of twelve section heads responded, and the results confirmed strong acceptance of the new dashboard and way of working.

Challenge

Because the usability testing was conducted on a functional product, implementing changes required additional development effort and careful prioritization. Beyond usability, it was equally important to understand whether users would actually adopt this new way of working.

Solution

Combine targeted usability sessions to identify the most impactful improvements before launch with a post-launch questionnaire to validate adoption, satisfaction and long-term acceptance of the new way of working.
